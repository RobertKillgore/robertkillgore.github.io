What went wrong for Kentucky basketball this season?

2021-04-02

The bluebloods of college basketball were not their usual selves in the 2020-2021 season, Kentucky being the most disappointing of them all. The Wildcats went 9-16 this year and finished 8-9 in conference play, missing the NCAA tournament for the first time since 2013. To begin looking at what went wrong for Kentucky this season, let’s start by grabbing the games logs for each team of the 2020-2021 season.

To get started, I’m going to look at cumulative point differential for the teams in the SEC. You would think a team with a positive point differential should be at least .500 while a team with a negative point differential should be below .500.

sec <- logs21 %>% 
  mutate(pointdifferential = TeamScore - OpponentScore) %>% group_by(Team) %>% mutate(CumPointDiff = cumsum(pointdifferential)) %>% filter(Conference == "SEC")

Now that we got the cumulative point differential for each team in the SEC, let’s compare Kentucky to the 22-6 Arkansas Razorbacks and the 13-14 Auburn Tigers.

uk <- sec %>% filter(Team == "Kentucky") 
ark <- sec %>% filter(Team == "Arkansas")
aub <- sec %>% filter(Team== "Auburn")
ggplot() + 
  geom_step(data=sec, aes(x=Date, y=CumPointDiff, group=Team), color="light grey") +
  geom_step(data=uk, aes(x=Date, y=CumPointDiff, group=Team), color="blue") + 
  geom_step(data=ark, aes(x=Date, y=CumPointDiff, group=Team), color="red") +
  geom_step(data=aub, aes(x=Date, y=CumPointDiff, color=Team), color="orange") +
  annotate("text", x=(as.Date("2021-01-02")), y=20, label="Auburn") +
  annotate("text", x=(as.Date("2021-1-16")), y=-30, label="Kentucky") +
  annotate("text", x=(as.Date("2021-02-01")), y=283, label="Arkansas") +
  labs(
    x="Date", 
    y="Cumulative Point Differential", 
    title="Kentucky's Inability to win close games",

    )

Kentucky ended up finishing the year with a positive point differential, something not typically seen in a 9-16 team. One reason for this is the Wildcats were 2-8 in games decided by 4 points or less, including a one point loss against Mississippi State in the SEC tournament. Say they go 5-5 instead, Kentucky ends up 12-13, a slightly below-average team.

Now, let’s look at Kentucky’s total turnovers and steals compared to the rest of the SEC. If a team forces a lot of turnovers in a given game, they steal away their opponents precious possessions and increase the likelihood of winning that game. Let’s see how well Kentucky kept care of the ball and how well they wrecked havoc on the defensive end.

I want to create a graph which highlights teams total turnovers and steals. I will use a dumbbell chart to show this, which shows the difference between two things on a number line. I’m going to make turnovers blue and steals purple. The total amount of turnovers and steals for each team will be on the x-axis and the y-axis will have the SEC teams, reordered by the amount of turnovers.

Kentucky ended up the year with less turnovers than top teams like Alabama and Arkansas, but had far less steals than them. Teams that made the tournament like LSU, Tennessee, and Alabama lines from steals and turnovers were smaller than Kentucky’s, showing how important turnover differential is to winning.

With the new information, we have let’s see how Kentucky underperformed this season. Let’s look at assists, a good metric for a team’s success. First let’s set up a scatterplot with the amount of wins each team had this year on the y-axis and team’s total assists on the x-axis

winlosslogs <- logs21 %>% 
  mutate(
    winloss = case_when(
      grepl("W", W_L) ~ 1, 
      grepl("L", W_L) ~ 0)
)
dimes <- winlosslogs %>% 
  group_by(Team) %>% 
  summarise(
    Wins = sum(winloss), 
    TotalAssists = sum(TeamAssists)
    )

Now we can create a scatterplot that shows the relationship between total assists and team wins. First, we have to find out what the R squared value is, which tells us how much assists factor in winning

fit <- lm(Wins ~ TotalAssists, data = dimes)
summary(fit)

The R squared value is 0.5134, which means that assists predicts about 51.34 percent of the variance in wins. That is a lot. I can also find out how teams underperformed or overperformed their win totals from the coefficients found above and the amount of assists a team had using the equation y=mx +b. Plugging in the numbers for Kentucky, it would like .050618 times 312(number of assists they had) + -3.630841. That equals 12.16, which means according to our model Kentucky should have won 12 games in the 2020-2021 season just looking at total assists. Let’s see how this looks in a scatterplot this season among teams in the NCAA this season, Kentucky the blue dot.

Kentucky played better than what their record said, the data showing they should have finished with 12 or 13 wins. It will be interesting to see if this year was just a fluke or a the start of the end of the Calipari Era in Lexington.
